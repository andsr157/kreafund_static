Why Burmaâ€¦Why now? It's the WINTER of 2023, and it certainly is NOT a good time to be hanging out in Burma. My name is Charlie Costello, and after six years, I am finally close to completing the book, Hanging Out in Burma: Laundry Lines and Democracy Hanging in the Balance. Just before my first visit in December 2011, the military began loosening its grip on the country. But when I arrived it still felt as if Big Brother was watching, that the authoritarian regime's oppressive rule still prevailed. This incredible country, the people and their cultural traditions and wisdom, the colors, the biodiversity, a country with so much to save - was calling out to me to Hang Out in Burma.Your support will help me complete the book and shine a light on BURMA amidst all of the things happening in the world. Balloons over Bagan, December 2011 Balloons over Bagan, December 2011 Burma is a country rich in mind, body, and soul, but sadly, a land that has been held hostage by its own military government. I want this project to tell my story, as I walk you through photos compiled over three trips, that will shine a light on the beauty, heart, and soul of the country and its people. Your support will pay homage to the strength and beauty of the people and their country and their quest for freedom that continues to this day. Charlie Costello in Bagan Charlie Costello in Bagan In November 2015, a national election was held and the National League for Democracy (NLD) won in a landslide. Later the following year, the winning party had a chance to appoint the president and key members of the government. I wasn't sure if it was real, so I traveled to Burma in February of 2016 to see for myself, with my feet on the ground, whether or not this election was for real. It seemed real. . . Rangoon fireworks Rangoon fireworks Fast forward five tumultuous years when another national election was held, and the NLD again, won in a landslide. Combine that with a world-wide pandemic and you have the unthinkable scenario. But the unthinkable happened, when, on February 1, 2021, a military coup d'etat was carried out under the premise of vote irregularity. More than two years have passed since that brutal military coup occurred and it's time to shine a light on the beauty, biodiversity, and people of this country. Sneak Peak - Sacred Numbers . . . Security Question Security Question - Juried Exhibit at the David Brower Center, Berkeley, CA This photograph was taken in February of 2014 in a village outside of Kalaw in the Shan State, after a festival when young boys become monks. As I walked through a neighboring village, I was struck by boys playing with guns, and the ease with which even a very young child carried what appeared to be a real gun judging by its weight, as if it were an extension of his arm. The future is unknown in Burma and around the world, and the hope is for a more mindful awareness of the despair and needless suffering, where compassion, gratitude and loving kindness rule the lands. This photo is also shown on page 108 of the book, the parting shot (pun intended) and the last page of the book for a reason. 108 is considered sacred in Hinduism, Buddhism, and Yogic traditions. Malas or Japa beads come in a string of 108 and are used for devotional meditation, mantra, on a baseball. Why Kickstarter? Short Answer: Because of the partnership with Blurb I chose to work with Kickstarter in order to raise money to self-publish this book. Self-publishing is not for everyone, but it gives authors, writers, and photographers a chance to get their work out into the world. Longer Answer: Kickstarter is an all-or-nothing fundraising platform, meaning that if you don't reach the initial fundraising goal by the end of your campaign, then the project does not get funded. I am not sure I would meet the requirements to get enough pre-orders in order to print the three versions in bulk, so I will be doing the fulfillment myself once the book is finalized. It also gives me a chance to bring awareness in support of the movement for democracy. About me I am a writer, photographer, and regenerative farmer, with a strong interest in community building and re-invigorating the concepts of local and global villages. I spent most of my life in the San Francisco Bay Area and recently relocated to a small community in the MidCoast of Maine. I have lived and worked in Japan and Thailand and have traveled extensively throughout Southeast Asia. Hanging out in Burma: Laundry Lines and Democracy Hanging in the Balance is the culmination of three journeys to Burma which progressively deepened my appreciation of the beauty and warmth of its people and the plight of its democracy. My photographs have been exhibited at the Berkeley Art Center, the David Brower Center, and the Tides Foundation. I have a BA in English from Santa Clara University and an MA in Asian Studies from the University of San Francisco. Hanging Out in Burma: Laundry Lines and Democracy Hanging in the Balance, is a personal project that I have been working on for the past six years. Things just kept on changing in Burma after the 2015 election and as a result, the book project kept getting stalled. It's time to shine the light on Burma, on the people, the colors, the beauty, and a country with so much to save. Gratitudes Special thanks to Peter Coates, an artist, and painter from Great Britain, who suggested the title after he and his wife Gill welcomed me to share a boat ride up the Kaladan river to the ancient kingdom of Mrauk-U. I'd also like to thank the following individuals for their ongoing support of my travel to and writing about Burma. Firstly, Arlene Blum for introducing me to the beauty of the people, the colors, and the diversity of Burma, leading my first trip and trek into Inle Lake and beyond. I have a number of very special friends in Burma and I want to thank them all for their support, friendship, and insights. I'd also like to thank my academic supporters and friends, especially Professor Uldis Kruze for his ongoing friendship, support and work on behalf of Asia. Professor Judith White, for her work on behalf of many causes in Burma including support of my work along the way. Professor John Nelson, for his continued encouragement and support of my writing and photography. University of San Francisco's Center for the Pacific Rim's founding Director Barbara Bundy and the late Professor Patrick Hatcher supported me while studying in the program. Professor Chiho Sawada of Holy Names University for his support and friendship on behalf of my work and photography. Lastly, I'd like to thank my wife, Liz Cunningham for her unending support of my writing, photography and travels to Burma.x

Risks and challenges

Quite unsurprisingly, the most foreseeable issue we may struggle with is the release date. While we optimistically set it to December 2024, we know from experience that a lot can go wrong overnight when you develop a video game. This media involves so many fields that it's hard to tell which one will cause trouble next! Additionally, we did not have a chance to delve too profoundly into the different console ports yet. Of course we shall do our best to release the game on as many systems and platforms as possible, but be wary that Liam_99 may not make it onto your favorite console! Learn about accountability on Kickstarter Environmental commitments Visit our Environmental Resources Center to learn how Kickstarter encourages sustainable practices. Long-lasting design All-in-one goal of the crowdfunding and core reward for the backers, the game should be playable for years. It's especially true as we propose the game DRM-free, which means PC players don't have to launch the game through platforms such as Steam or Epic Games. Regarding physical rewards: - We'll work with a local print shop to bring the paper posters to life. This shall allow us to check quality and minimize environmental costs linked to transport. - The T-shirts will be produced by a French company. We know from our own experience that they work with lasting materials. - The metallic posters will be handled by Displate. It's super sturdy, looks super cool, and the society works with communities in Africa to plant trees and improve the locals' quality of life.
